The Hands

Artisans

Every DYUSK pattern draws on centuries of Indian craft. These are the heritage regions our ideas come from.

  • Three centuries of hand-carved block printing.

    Jaipur's hand-carved wooden blocks have stamped cloth for over three hundred years. The same geometric discipline — repeat, register, hold the line — shows up in how we grade a compression panel.

  • A village built entirely around block printing.

    A village built entirely around block printing, still working in natural dyes and hand-carved teak blocks. We borrow its patience — nothing ships until the repeat is perfect.

  • Ajrakh's sixteen-step resist-dye tradition.

    Ajrakh's resist-dye process runs up to sixteen steps before a single print is finished. It's the same standard we hold our own six-zone pressure map to — no step skipped.

  • Banarasi brocade, woven over weeks by hand.

    Banarasi weavers can spend weeks on a single length of brocade. That obsession with a seam nobody else will notice is exactly the standard our flatlock operators work to.

  • Silk woven with an almost invisible seam.

    Kanchipuram silk is woven with body, border and pallu dyed separately, then joined so precisely the seam nearly disappears — the same invisible-seam standard we hold flatlock work to.

  • Handloom fine enough to feel translucent.

    Chanderi handloom is famous for a fabric light enough to feel translucent without losing structure. Compression asks the opposite question — how much can a panel hold — but the same precision applies.

  • Kashmiri embroidery, worked entirely by hand.

    Kashmiri embroidery is worked entirely by hand, needle by needle, over months. It's a reminder that engineered doesn't have to mean machine-only — our own finishing is still done by hand too.
